Category I: Acute Bacterial Prostatitis
‚úì Use this if patient has:
  • Sudden onset (hours to days)
  • Fever, chills, or feeling systemically unwell
  • Severe pelvic/perineal pain
  • Acute urinary symptoms (dysuria, frequency, urgency)
  • Positive urine culture
üí° This is a medical emergency if septic
Category II: Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis
‚úì Use this if patient has:
  • Symptoms for ‚â•3 months
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections (same organism)
  • Pelvic pain/discomfort (less severe than acute)
  • Positive urine/prostatic fluid culture
  • Symptoms improve with antibiotics but return when stopped
üí° Bacteria proven in prostate - needs prolonged antibiotics
Category III: CP/CPPS (Most Common - 90-95%)
‚úì Use this if patient has:
  • Chronic pelvic pain for ‚â•3 months
  • Lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS)
  • Negative urine cultures (no bacteria)
  • No history of recurrent UTIs
  • Pain in pelvis, perineum, genitals, or lower back
üí° Diagnosis of exclusion - no infection found, needs multimodal therapy
Category IV: Asymptomatic
‚úì Use this if patient has:
  • NO symptoms at all
  • Inflammation found incidentally on:
  • - Prostate biopsy (done for elevated PSA)
  • - Semen analysis (investigating infertility)
  • - Other investigations
üí° Usually needs NO treatment - just observation
Quick Decision Tree:
1. Is patient symptomatic? ‚Üí NO = Category IV
2. Are symptoms acute (<6 weeks) with fever? ‚Üí YES = Category I
3. Is urine/prostatic culture positive? ‚Üí YES = Category II
4. Chronic symptoms (>3 months) with negative cultures? ‚Üí Category III

Category I: Acute Bacterial Prostatitis

‚ö†Ô∏è Clinical Emergency: May present with sepsis. Consider hospital admission for severe cases.
Essential Investigations EAU
  • Digital rectal examination (gentle - avoid vigorous massage)
  • Urine dipstick and microscopy
  • Midstream urine culture and sensitivity
  • Blood cultures if febrile/systemically unwell
  • FBC, CRP, U&E
  • PSA (defer if possible - elevated in acute inflammation)

First-line Antibiotic Therapy NICEBNF

Ciprofloxacin (First choice if not pregnant)
500mg BD PO for 14 days (acute uncomplicated)
500mg BD PO for 28 days (severe/complicated)
Excellent prostatic penetration. Check for quinolone resistance locally.
Levofloxacin (Alternative)
500mg OD PO for 14-28 days
Alternative fluoroquinolone with good penetration.
Trimethoprim (If quinolones contraindicated)
200mg BD PO for 14-28 days
Lower resistance rates in some areas. Good prostatic penetration.
Co-amoxiclav (If Gram-positive suspected)
625mg TDS PO for 14-28 days
Consider if enterococcal infection suspected or culture-proven.

Severe Cases Requiring Admission EAU

Gentamicin + Amoxicillin (IV)
Gentamicin 5-7mg/kg OD IV + Amoxicillin 1g TDS IV
For sepsis/severely unwell. Switch to oral when improving (minimum 2 weeks total).
Ceftriaxone (Alternative IV)
2g OD IV
Alternative broad-spectrum option. Poor oral bioavailability requires prolonged IV.

Supportive Management

  • Analgesia: Paracetamol 1g QDS + Ibuprofen 400mg TDS (if no contraindications)
  • Alpha-blocker: Tamsulosin 400mcg OD (for urinary symptoms, continue 4-6 weeks)
  • Adequate hydration (encourage oral fluids)
  • Stool softeners if constipated (avoid straining)
  • Catheterization: Suprapubic if urinary retention (avoid urethral catheter if possible)
üö® RED FLAGS - Immediate Hospital Referral
  • Sepsis (fever >38¬∞C, tachycardia, hypotension)
  • Acute urinary retention requiring catheterization
  • Inability to tolerate oral medications
  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Prostatic abscess suspected (fluctuant prostate on DRE)

Category II: Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis

Definition: Recurrent UTIs caused by prostatic focus of infection. Symptoms ‚â•3 months.
Diagnostic Investigations EAU
  • 4-glass Meares-Stamey test (gold standard) or 2-glass pre/post-massage urine test
  • Expressed prostatic secretions (EPS) microscopy and culture
  • Midstream urine culture (MSU) - repeated samples
  • PSA (often mildly elevated)
  • Consider transrectal ultrasound (TRUS) if recurrent/resistant
  • Urodynamics if voiding dysfunction suspected

First-line Prolonged Antibiotic Therapy NICE4-6 weeks

Ciprofloxacin (First choice)
500mg BD PO for 4-6 weeks
Best prostatic penetration. Superior to other antibiotics for chronic bacterial prostatitis.
Levofloxacin
500mg OD PO for 4-6 weeks
Alternative fluoroquinolone. Once-daily dosing may improve compliance.
Trimethoprim
200mg BD PO for 4-6 weeks
Second-line. Use if fluoroquinolones contraindicated or organism sensitive.
Doxycycline (For atypical organisms)
100mg BD PO for 4-6 weeks
Consider for Chlamydia or Mycoplasma. Requires STI screening context.

Adjunctive Therapy GIRFT

Alpha-blockers
Tamsulosin 400mcg OD or Alfuzosin 10mg OD
Continue for 3-6 months. Improves voiding symptoms and may enhance antibiotic penetration.
Anti-inflammatory
Ibuprofen 400mg TDS (with food) PRN
For symptomatic relief. Limit to 2-4 weeks continuous use.

Management of Recurrent Infection

  • Culture-directed antibiotic therapy based on sensitivity
  • Consider longer duration (3 months) for persistent infection
  • Low-dose prophylactic antibiotics (e.g., Trimethoprim 100mg ON) after completing treatment
  • Evaluate for bladder calculi or structural abnormalities (cystoscopy/TRUS)
  • Consider urology referral for resistant cases

üåø Complementary Approaches (Limited evidence)

  • Quercetin: 500mg BD (anti-inflammatory bioflavonoid - some RCT evidence)
  • Saw Palmetto: 320mg daily (traditional use, mixed evidence)
  • Pelvic floor physiotherapy: May help if muscle dysfunction component
  • Hydration: Maintain good fluid intake (2L/day)

Category III: Chronic Prostatitis/Chronic Pelvic Pain Syndrome (CP/CPPS)

Most Common Type: 90-95% of chronic prostatitis cases. No bacterial infection identified.
Subtypes: IIIa (inflammatory - white cells in EPS) | IIIb (non-inflammatory)
Diagnostic Approach EAU
  • Diagnosis of exclusion - rule out bacterial infection
  • Urine culture (should be negative)
  • Expressed prostatic secretions (EPS) - may show WBCs in IIIa
  • NIH-Chronic Prostatitis Symptom Index (NIH-CPSI) - quantify symptoms
  • Consider STI screen (Chlamydia, Gonorrhoea, Mycoplasma)
  • PSA (usually normal or mildly elevated)
  • Post-void residual urine volume
  • Uroflowmetry ¬± urodynamics if voiding dysfunction

Multimodal Management Strategy NICEEAU

‚ö†Ô∏è Note: This is a challenging condition requiring individualized, multimodal approach. No single therapy is universally effective.

1. Pharmacological Options

Alpha-blockers (First-line for LUTS)
Tamsulosin 400mcg OD or Alfuzosin 10mg OD
Trial for 4-6 weeks initially. Continue if effective for 3-6 months. Best for newly diagnosed patients with LUTS.
NSAIDs (Anti-inflammatory)
Ibuprofen 400mg TDS with food (max 2-4 weeks continuous)
Or Naproxen 500mg BD. Limit duration due to GI/cardiovascular risks.
Pregabalin (Neuropathic pain component)
Start 75mg BD, increase to 150-300mg BD as tolerated
For patients with significant pelvic pain. Titrate slowly. May cause dizziness/sedation.
Amitriptyline (Alternative for pain)
Start 10mg ON, increase to 25-75mg ON
Low-dose tricyclic for chronic pain. May also help with sleep/bladder symptoms.
Antibiotics (Trial in selected cases)
Doxycycline 100mg BD for 4-6 weeks OR Ciprofloxacin 500mg BD for 4-6 weeks
Consider if atypical organisms suspected or inflammatory subtype (IIIa). Limited evidence but may benefit some patients.
5-alpha reductase inhibitors (For enlarged prostate)
Finasteride 5mg OD
Consider if prostate >40g and LUTS prominent. Requires 6-12 months trial. Sexual side effects possible.

2. Pelvic Floor Physiotherapy EAU

  • Myofascial trigger point release: Specialist pelvic physio assessment
  • Pelvic floor relaxation exercises: Paradoxical pelvic floor tension common
  • Biofeedback therapy: To retrain pelvic floor muscles
  • Course: Typically 8-12 sessions over 3-6 months
  • Evidence: Moderate-quality evidence for symptom improvement

Follow-up Strategy GIRFT

  • Review at 4-6 weeks with NIH-CPSI score reassessment
  • Trial one intervention at a time to assess efficacy
  • Consider urology referral if refractory to initial management
  • Specialist pain clinic referral for severe, persistent pain
  • Realistic expectation setting - aim for symptom improvement rather than cure
üí° Clinical Pearl: CP/CPPS is heterogeneous. What works for one patient may not work for another. Adopt a pragmatic, stepwise approach trialing different modalities. Combination therapy often more effective than monotherapy.

Category IV: Asymptomatic Inflammatory Prostatitis

Definition: Incidental finding of prostatic inflammation without symptoms. Usually discovered during:
  • Evaluation for infertility (semen analysis showing leukocytes)
  • Elevated PSA investigation (prostate biopsy)
  • Investigation for other urological conditions
Clinical Characteristics
  • No symptoms: No pain, no LUTS, no sexual dysfunction
  • Findings: Elevated WBC in prostatic secretions/semen or inflammation on biopsy
  • PSA: May be elevated due to inflammation
  • Prevalence: Common incidental finding

Management Approach EAU

‚úì Key Principle: Generally does NOT require treatment as patient is asymptomatic.

Observation Strategy

  • Reassure patient - benign condition, no intervention needed
  • No antibiotic therapy indicated
  • No anti-inflammatory therapy needed
  • Monitor PSA if initially elevated - expect normalization over time

Special Scenarios Requiring Intervention

1. Infertility Context
If significant leukocytospermia affecting fertility:
‚Ä¢ Consider trial of antibiotics (e.g., Doxycycline 100mg BD for 4 weeks)
‚Ä¢ Antioxidant supplements
‚Ä¢ Refer to fertility specialist for comprehensive evaluation
2. Persistently Elevated PSA
If PSA remains elevated after 6-12 weeks:
‚Ä¢ Repeat PSA after 4-6 weeks (may normalize spontaneously)
‚Ä¢ Consider MRI prostate if PSA remains elevated
‚Ä¢ Standard prostate cancer screening pathway if age-appropriate
‚Ä¢ Trial of antibiotics NOT recommended just to reduce PSA
3. Recurrent UTIs (Uncommon)
If patient develops recurrent UTIs:
‚Ä¢ Treat as Category II chronic bacterial prostatitis
‚Ä¢ Culture-directed antibiotic therapy
‚Ä¢ Reclassify to symptomatic category
Follow-up Recommendations NICE
  • No routine follow-up needed for asymptomatic patients
  • If PSA elevated: Repeat PSA at 6 weeks and 3 months
  • Patient education: Advise to report if symptoms develop
  • No restrictions on lifestyle or activity
  • Does not increase prostate cancer risk
‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Do NOT treat asymptomatic patients with antibiotics "just in case" or to reduce PSA. This contributes to antibiotic resistance without clinical benefit.

When to Refer to Urology

  • Persistently elevated PSA despite observation (>6 months)
  • PSA continues to rise on serial measurements
  • Abnormal DRE findings
  • Patient develops symptoms (reclassify to appropriate category)
  • Infertility workup requiring specialist input
